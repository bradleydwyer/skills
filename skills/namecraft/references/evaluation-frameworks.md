## SMILE/SCRATCH Framework

Developed by Alexandra Watkins (Eat My Words agency). Industry-standard rapid evaluation.

### SMILE — Positive Qualities (aim for all 5)

| Criterion | Definition | How to Score | Example |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Suggestive** | Evokes a positive brand attribute without being literal | Does the name hint at what the product does or how it feels? | Kindle suggests igniting passion for reading |
| **Memorable** | Anchored in familiar associations that aid recall | Is there a mental hook? A visual, a feeling, a reference? | Amazon — vastness is immediately graspable |
| **Imagery** | Conjures a distinct visual in the mind | Can you picture something when you hear it? | Nest — instant image of home warmth |
| **Legs** | Extensible for marketing campaigns and sub-products | Can you build a theme around it? Does it support a brand world? | Apple — orchards, seeds, branches, etc. |
| **Emotional** | Creates a psychological connection, not just data | Does it make you feel something? | Liquid Death — immediate visceral reaction |

### SCRATCH — Deal-Breakers (any hit is a red flag)

| Criterion | Definition | Test | Example Failures |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Spelling-challenged** | Looks like a typo or requires clarification | Say it aloud. Can someone spell it? | Flickr (is it Flicker? Flickr?) |
| **Copycat** | Too similar to an existing brand | Would someone confuse it with a competitor? | Naming a chat app "Slak" |
| **Restrictive** | Limits future growth | Could the company pivot without a rebrand? | "ServerStack" for a cloud company |
| **Annoying** | Forced, try-hard, or grating | Does it feel natural or contrived? | Excessive puns, forced cleverness |
| **Tame** | Flat, descriptive, uninspired | Does it stand out from generic alternatives? | "SmartAnalytics Pro" |
| **Curse of knowledge** | Insider reference the public won't get | Would your non-technical friend understand? | Obscure mythology only classicists know |
| **Hard to pronounce** | Blocks word-of-mouth marketing | Can people say it after reading it once? | "Axicabtagene ciloleucel" |

## Radio Test

A critical heuristic: **If someone hears this name spoken aloud once — on a podcast, in conversation, at a conference — can they spell it correctly and find it in a search engine?**

Names that fail the Radio Test:
- Include numbers where digit vs word is ambiguous ("4" or "four"?)
- Use hyphens or underscores
- Rely on non-standard spelling (Lyft, Flickr — is it -er or -r?)
- Have multiple plausible spellings (Kustomer — C or K?)

The Radio Test directly impacts Customer Acquisition Cost. Every fraction of a second of spelling friction is a lost conversion.

**Quick check**: Say the name to someone who hasn't seen it written. Ask them to type it. If they can't get it right on the first try, the name fails.

## 5-Year Suitability Test

Project the name 5 years into the future:

1. **Geographic expansion**: Does it work internationally? Any negative meanings in major languages?
2. **Product line diversification**: If the company adds new products, does the name still fit?
3. **Demographic shifts**: Will the target audience evolve? Does the name scale?
4. **Technology cycles**: Is the name tied to a trend that may become dated? (Cloud-, AI-, NFT-)
5. **Cultural relevance**: Could external events taint the name? (like "Ayds" candy during the AIDS epidemic, or "Covid" electronics)

### Linguistic Disaster Check

Mandatory for any name that will be used internationally:
- Check phonetic meaning in major languages (Mandarin, Spanish, Arabic, Hindi, Japanese, Portuguese)
- Ford Pinto = male genitalia in Brazilian Portuguese
- "Huruhuru" beer = "pubic hair" in Maori (intended: "light as a feather")
- Head & Shoulders successfully localized to Chinese as 海飞丝 (hǎi fēi sī = "flying silk of the sea")

## Anti-Pattern Catalog

### Overused Suffixes (signal "follower")
- **-ify**: 187+ companies tracked. Once innovative (Spotify, 2006), now derivative. Includes absurdities like Employify, Knowify, Debuggify.
- **-ly**: Single most common suffix in YC portfolio. Bitly, Grammarly, Calendly — heavily saturated.
- **-hub**: GitHub, Grubhub, HubSpot — now generic.
- **-io**: Tied to .io TLD. Developer tool signal, but overused.
- **-ai**: Post-2023 gold rush. Will date quickly as AI becomes ambient.

### Compound Wallflowers
Igor identifies these overused component words. Combining any two produces forgettable names:

> Active, Arc, Atlas, Blue, Bridge, Care, Clear, Complete, Core, Curve, Edge, Engage, Flow, Force, Fusion, Future, Hub, Next, Path, Plus, Pulse, Shift, Sky, Smart, Stream, Wave, Wise, Zip

SmartFlow, CorePulse, NexPoint, EdgeForce — indistinguishable from hundreds of competitors. These are "forgotten in a heartbeat."

### Dated Conventions
- **Dropped vowels** (Flickr-style): Signals 2007, not 2026
- **Creative misspellings** (Lyft, Fiverr): "People who hear the name won't know the unusual spelling"
- **e- and i- prefixes**: eBay, iPhone — Apple moved away from "i" after 2010
- **Tech buzzwords**: Cloud-, Cyber-, Blockchain-, Web3-, NFT- — memorialize trends that become passe

### Other Anti-Patterns
- **Puns**: "Amusing at the moment — then they're like vapor — they're gone" (Steve Manning, Igor)
- **Overused human names**: Multiple funded startups named Oscar, Mighty, Magic, Wise, or Ada
- **Initials/acronyms for new brands**: Harder to memorize and differentiate. IBM earned its abbreviation over decades.
- **Amazon killed baby names**: Alexa's popularity plummeted after Amazon's launch. Be aware of name collisions with popular products.

## Professional Agency Methods

### Lexicon Branding
Creators of BlackBerry, Pentium, Swiffer, Sonos, Dasani, Impossible Foods. ~$150k per project.

**Core method — "Surprisingly Familiar"**: Take familiar concepts and place them in unexpected contexts. BlackBerry = friendly fruit + enterprise security device. The cognitive friction makes it memorable while the familiar word keeps it approachable.

**Three-team process**: One team knows the real brief. One thinks they're naming a competitor. One works on an unrelated category. Prevents groupthink. Generate 1,000-1,500+ names before shortlisting 50-100.

### Igor International
Creators of truTV, Gogo Inflight, Aria Las Vegas.

**Four name categories**:
1. Functional/Descriptive
2. Invented
3. Experiential
4. Evocative (rarest, most powerful — "Apple for computers, Virgin for airlines")

**Key warning**: Avoid "Compound Wallflowers" — combinations of overused words that feel like white noise.

### Catchword
Creators of Asana, Upwork, Vudu.

**Key insight**: A name can be a constraint or a growth vessel. Naming research popularity contests are just data points — the most familiar names test highest in focus groups but fail to differentiate.

### Key Research Findings
- Single English words generate the most consumer curiosity (Atom.com survey)
- 77% of consumers say difficult-to-spell names impact brand credibility
- The Von Restorff effect: distinctive items remembered up to 30x better
- Consumers pay 22% more for brands perceived as "different"

## App Store Naming Constraints

If the product is a mobile app, these platform rules are non-negotiable.

### Character Limits
- **App Title**: 30 characters (both iOS and Android)
- **Subtitle** (iOS): 30 characters
- **Keyword Field** (iOS only): 100 characters (hidden, indexed)

### Best Practices
- Allocate 10-15 characters for the brand name, leaving room for discovery keywords
- Use "Brand - Description" format: "Calm - Meditation & Sleep", "Notion - Notes, Tasks, AI"
- Ampersands save characters over "and"
- Don't duplicate keywords across Title, Subtitle, and Keyword fields
- Both stores prohibit emojis in app names

### Platform Differences
- **Apple**: Hidden 100-char keyword field indexed for search. Description NOT indexed.
- **Google Play**: No keyword field. Indexes title, short description (80 chars), and full description (4,000 chars).

## Domain Strategy

The skill does NOT check domain availability, but understanding TLD signals helps name selection.

### TLD Signal Value
- **.com**: Still commands 30-40% memorability premium. 78% of funded startups use it.
- **.ai**: Category descriptor for AI companies. 23% of YC W24 batch. $32M revenue for Anguilla.
- **.io**: Developer tools signal ("Input/Output"). 1.6M registrations by 2025.
- **.app**: Forces HTTPS. Signals mobile-first.
- **.dev**: Developer-focused, also forces HTTPS.

### Common .com Workarounds
When the exact .com isn't available, funded startups commonly use:
- **"get" prefix** (26.1%): getballoon.com
- **"app" suffix** (22.6%): notionapp.com
- **"go" prefix** (11.35%): gobudget.com
- **"the" prefix** (8.7%): thelinear.com
- **"try" or "use" prefix**: trynotion.com, usejournal.com

### Key Insight
Choose the name first, then solve the domain problem. The name is the brand — the domain is logistics.
