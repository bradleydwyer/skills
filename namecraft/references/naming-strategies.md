## Strategic Intent Spectrum

The Abdi-Meyerson spectrum classifies names by how directly they describe the product. Moving from left to right increases trademark strength but requires more marketing investment.

### Generic
Identifies an entire product class. Cannot be trademarked.
- Hotels.com, Cars.com, Calculator, Weather, Booking.com, Dictionary.com

### Descriptive
Directly describes the product or its attributes. Immediately clear but legally weak.
- PayPal ("pay a pal"), YouTube ("personal television"), GameStop, Pizza Hut, E-Trade
- **Subtypes**: Founder names (McKinsey, Dell, Bloomberg), Geographic (Palo Alto Networks, Silicon Valley Bank)

### Suggestive (the sweet spot)
Hints at qualities through metaphor without directly describing them. Best balance of communication and trademarkability.
- Slack (relaxation/ease), Amazon (vastness), Kindle (igniting reading), Zoom (speed)
- Robinhood (democratizing finance), Nest (home comfort), Buffer, Sprint, Pinterest

### Arbitrary
Real dictionary words in completely unrelated contexts. Strong trademark protection.
- Apple (computers), Shell (gasoline), Blackberry (smartphones), Virgin (airlines)
- Caterpillar (construction), Patagonia (clothing), Uber ("above/beyond" for rideshare)

### Fanciful/Coined
Invented words with no prior meaning. Strongest legal protection, highest marketing cost.
- Google, Xerox, Kodak, Spotify, Hulu, Skype, Verizon ("veritas" + "horizon")
- Dasani, Haagen-Dazs (completely fictional Scandinavian-sounding)

## Linguistic Construction Types

| Construction | Technique | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| Real words (unmodified) | Dictionary words as-is | Slack, Stripe, Square, Notion, Linear, Signal, Snap |
| Modified/misspelled | Deliberate alterations | Lyft, Fiverr, Tumblr, Flickr, Reddit, Dribbble, Xero |
| Compound words | Two words fused | Facebook, YouTube, Snapchat, Dropbox, WordPress, LinkedIn |
| Portmanteaus | Parts of two words merged | Instagram, Pinterest, Microsoft, Groupon, Netflix, Accenture |
| Invented/coined | Created from scratch | Google, Hulu, Kodak, Spotify, Etsy, Zapier, Twilio |
| Acronyms/initialisms | Abbreviated from phrases | IBM, AWS, SAP, BMW, HP, HTC |
| Clipped/truncated | Shortened words | Cisco (San Francisco), Intel (Integrated Electronics), FedEx |
| Foreign language | Non-English words | Roku ("six" in Japanese), Samsung ("three stars" in Korean), Ubuntu |
| Suffix additions | Appended morphemes | -ify (Spotify, Shopify), -ly (Grammarly, Bitly), -hub (GitHub) |
| Prefix additions | Prepended morphemes | e- (eBay), i- (iPhone), un- (Unbounce), re- (Replit, Retool) |

## Psycholinguistic Principles

### Sound Symbolism (Bouba/Kiki Effect)

95-98% of people across cultures associate "bouba" with rounded shapes and "kiki" with angular ones. This maps directly to brand perception:

**Soft consonants (b, m, l) + rounded vowels (o, u)** = friendly, smooth, reliable
- Google, Coca-Cola, Dove, Oatly

**Hard consonants (k, t, p) + front vowels (i, e)** = analytical, precise, modern
- Kodak, TikTok, Nike, Stripe

### Consonant Categories and Their Signals

| Category | Sounds | Perception |
|---|---|---|
| Voiceless stops | k, p, t | Fast, sharp, crisp, dynamic |
| Fricatives | f, v, s, z | Energetic, high-speed, velocity |
| Voiced consonants | b, d, g | Heavy, dominant, powerful |
| Liquids/soft | l, m, r | Smooth, calm, luxurious, elegant |

### Vowel Positioning

- **Front vowels** (/i/, /e/) = smallness, lightness, speed — good for lightweight tools
- **Back vowels** (/o/, /u/) = largeness, power, luxury — good for premium/enterprise
- Vowels drive brand meaning more strongly than consonants in blended brand names

### Specific Letter Associations

- **V** conjures energy and aliveness (Corvette, Visa, Vim)
- **B** and **T** convey reliability (BlackBerry, Toyota)
- **S** feels sleek (Samsung, Sony)
- **K** is "a strong, incisive sort of letter" (George Eastman on Kodak)

### Processing Fluency

Easy-to-process names trigger positive affect at a psychophysiological level. Companies with fluent stock tickers outperform those with complex ones immediately after IPO.

### Optimal Structure

- **1-3 syllables, 5-12 letters** is the sweet spot
- **Trochee pattern** (stressed-unstressed: STAR-bucks, AP-ple) feels natural in English
- **Phonetic repetition** increases favorability (Kit Kat, TikTok, Tic Tac, Coca-Cola)
- Names with inward mouth movement (front to back articulation) are preferred — mimics swallowing (positive) vs spitting (negative)

## Historical Context by Era

Understanding what's current vs dated helps avoid naming anti-patterns.

| Era | Period | Dominant Patterns | Status in 2026 |
|---|---|---|---|
| Dot-com | 1995-2002 | e- prefix, .com in name, extreme literalism | Deeply dated |
| Web 2.0 | 2004-2009 | Dropped vowels, misspellings, made-up words | Dated |
| Suffix era | 2008-2015 | -ify, -ly, -hub, -able, -io | Oversaturated |
| App boom | 2011-2018 | Single real words, nature metaphors, verbs | Still viable |
| Late consolidation | 2017-2022 | Alternative TLDs, premium domains | Current |
| AI explosion | 2023-present | Labs suffix, human names, .ai, mythological | Current but saturating |

### Current Landscape (2024-2026)

- "Labs" appears in 1-in-20 AI companies
- .ai registrations surged 375% post-ChatGPT (610,000+ by 2025)
- Average AI company name is 10.6 characters
- Most-cited companies skew short: Cohere (6), Cursor (6), Pika (4), Suno (4)
- Counter-trend toward ultra-simple dictionary words and nature metaphors
- Branding agencies predict: cozy/community names, "honest/transparent" names, bold stance-taking names

### What's Becoming Dated

- Reflexive "AI" appending
- "Deep" prefix (DeepMind was first; now diluted)
- "GPT" in names
- Creative misspellings
- Tech buzzword prefixes (Cloud-, Cyber-, Blockchain-)
